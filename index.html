Abstract

Large multimodal models, capable of processing and integrating diverse data types such as text and images, offer substantial benefits in tasks requiring complex data interpretation and human-like reasoning, demonstrating significant potential as visual assistants across various domains. Video game bug detection presents unique challenges that traditional software testing methods struggle to address, particularly in reasoning about logical inconsistencies rather than just technical errors. In this paper, we introduce BugsBunny, a large multimodal model designed for video game bug detection. BugsBunny can detect issues that are not flagged by traditional software engineering error signals, such as illogical behaviors within game environments (e.g., horses flying in the air or characters walking through solid walls). Our comprehensive study explores the potential application of BugsBunny in real-world bug detection scenarios, tested on a dataset of 500 images of video game bugs collected from various sources, and compares its performance with state-of-the-art proprietary models such as GPT-4o and Gemini-1.5-Pro.
